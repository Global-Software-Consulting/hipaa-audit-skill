02 HIPAA Complete Guide

What is HIPAA?

Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act — US federal law signed 1996 that protects sensitive patient health information from disclosure without consent.

Two main goals

  1. Portability — workers keep health insurance when changing jobs
  2. Accountability — set national standards for protecting health information

Why it matters today

  • Healthcare data breaches cost $10.93M average (IBM 2023 report) — highest of any industry
  • OCR enforcement up 250% over last decade
  • Cyber attacks on healthcare up 74% in 2023
  • Every state has additional health-data laws stacking on top

Quick definition

HIPAA = the rules every entity touching protected health information (PHI) must follow.

If you handle health data of US patients, HIPAA applies — even if your company is foreign.

Enforced by

  • HHS Office for Civil Rights (OCR) — federal enforcement
  • State Attorneys General — can sue independently
  • Department of Justice — criminal cases
03 HIPAA Complete Guide

History & timeline

1996HIPAA enacted by Congress. Initially focused on insurance portability.
2003Privacy Rule finalized. Sets standards for use and disclosure of PHI.
2005Security Rule finalized. Sets standards for electronic PHI (ePHI).
2009HITECH Act expanded HIPAA — added breach notification, increased penalties, extended to Business Associates directly.
2013Omnibus Final Rule — strengthened BAA requirements, broadened "breach" definition (burden of proof shifted to covered entity).
2024Tracking Technologies Bulletin (Dec) — OCR clarified Google Analytics / Meta Pixel on patient pages = HIPAA violation.
2024NIST 800-66 Rev 2 published — current implementation guide for the Security Rule.
2025HIPAA Security Rule NPRM proposed — mandatory MFA, encryption, vulnerability scans, network segmentation. Expected finalization 2026–2027.
04 HIPAA Complete Guide

Who must comply — Covered Entities

HIPAA directly binds Covered Entities (CEs) — three categories defined by law.

1. Healthcare Providers

Any provider who transmits health info electronically in connection with a HIPAA-defined transaction.

  • Hospitals, clinics
  • Physicians, dentists, chiropractors
  • Pharmacies
  • Mental health providers
  • Nursing homes
  • Home health agencies

2. Health Plans

Entity that provides or pays for medical care.

  • Health insurance companies
  • HMOs
  • Employer-sponsored group plans
  • Government plans (Medicare, Medicaid, VA)
  • Dental, vision, prescription drug plans

3. Healthcare Clearinghouses

Entities that process health info between providers and payers.

  • Billing services
  • Repricing companies
  • Community health management info systems

Less common; included for completeness.

Not covered by HIPAA

Life insurance, workers' comp, employer health records (employment use), most fitness/wellness apps, consumer DNA tests. They have other laws (FTC Act, state laws, GDPR).

05 HIPAA Complete Guide

Who must comply — Business Associates

Business Associate (BA) = any person or entity performing services for a Covered Entity that involves use or disclosure of PHI.

Examples of Business Associates

  • Cloud hosting providers (AWS, GCP, Azure)
  • SaaS platforms processing patient data
  • Software developers building EHR systems
  • Email services (Mailgun, SendGrid)
  • Error monitoring (Sentry, Datadog) — Enterprise tier
  • Billing & claims processing
  • Lawyers, accountants, consultants with PHI access
  • Medical transcription services
  • Records storage / shredding companies
  • Subcontractors of any BA (cascade)

HITECH 2009 expansion

Before HITECH, only Covered Entities could be fined. Now BAs are directly liable to OCR for HIPAA violations — same penalty tiers as CEs. Subcontractors of BAs also liable. The "BAA chain" can go 3+ levels deep.

If you build healthcare software

You are almost certainly a Business Associate. You need a signed BAA with every Covered Entity client AND every subprocessor you use that touches PHI. No BAA + PHI flow = automatic violation.

06 HIPAA Complete Guide

What is PHI?

Protected Health Information (PHI) = any information about health status, healthcare, or payment for healthcare that can be linked to an individual.

The formula

PHI = Health Information + Identifier

Either alone is not PHI. Combined, it is.

Examples of PHI

  • Patient's name + diagnosis
  • Email + prescription
  • Phone number + appointment date
  • Photo of patient + medical record
  • IP address + visit to clinic website
  • Insurance ID + treatment claim

ePHI (electronic PHI)

PHI created, stored, transmitted, or maintained electronically. Security Rule applies specifically to ePHI.

  • EHR / EMR records
  • Lab results in databases
  • Emails containing patient info
  • SMS, push notifications, app data
  • Backup tapes, cloud storage
  • Logs that capture PHI

Designated Record Set (DRS)

The complete set of medical/billing records about a patient — what patients have right to access.

07 HIPAA Complete Guide

The 18 Safe Harbor identifiers

Per 45 CFR § 164.514(b)(2). Remove ALL 18 → data is de-identified → HIPAA no longer applies.

  1. Names
  2. Geographic info smaller than state (street, city, county, ZIP)
  3. Dates except year (birth, admission, discharge, death, service)
  4. Phone numbers
  5. Fax numbers
  6. Email addresses
  7. Social Security numbers
  8. Medical record numbers (MRN)
  9. Health plan beneficiary numbers
  1. Account numbers
  2. Certificate / license numbers
  3. Vehicle identifiers (license plates, VIN)
  4. Device identifiers and serial numbers
  5. Web URLs identifying individuals
  6. IP addresses
  7. Biometric identifiers (fingerprints, voiceprints, retinal scans)
  8. Full-face photographs and comparable images
  9. Any other unique identifying number, characteristic, or code

Common gotchas

  • Black bars over eyes ≠ sufficient — full face means face anatomy, not just identifying gaze
  • For patients > 89, year must be aggregated to "90+" — birth year reveals age
  • Rare disease + ZIP + age can re-identify even without name (statistical disclosure)
  • Initials are identifiers in small populations

09 HIPAA Complete Guide

Privacy Rule — overview

Effective 2003. Sets national standards for use and disclosure of PHI in any form (paper, oral, electronic).

Core principle: Minimum Necessary

When using or disclosing PHI, limit to the minimum needed for the purpose.

Does NOT apply to:

  • Treatment (providers may need everything)
  • Patient-authorized disclosures
  • Disclosures to the patient themselves
  • Required by law (subpoenas, public health)
  • HHS compliance investigations

Permitted uses without authorization (TPO)

  1. Treatment — providing care
  2. Payment — billing, claims
  3. Operations — quality assessment, training, audits

Requires written authorization

  • Research (unless IRB waiver)
  • Marketing
  • Sale of PHI
  • Psychotherapy notes
  • Any use beyond TPO

Notice of Privacy Practices (NPP)

Every Covered Entity must publish a written notice describing how PHI is used, patient rights, and how to file complaints. Posted on website + given to patients at first encounter.

10 HIPAA Complete Guide

Privacy Rule — patient rights

Six rights every patient has under HIPAA. Covered Entities must support all six.

1. Right of Access

Get a copy of their PHI in the requested format within 30 days. Electronic copy must be in machine-readable format.

2. Right to Amend

Request corrections to PHI they believe is inaccurate or incomplete. CE can deny but must give reason.

3. Accounting of Disclosures

Get a list of every non-TPO disclosure of their PHI in the past 6 years.

4. Request Restrictions

Ask CE to limit use/disclosure (e.g., don't share with specific family member). CE may decline except for self-pay-only restriction.

5. Confidential Communications

Receive PHI via alternative means (work address vs home, alternate phone).

6. File Complaints

Complain to the CE or directly to HHS OCR — without retaliation.

Cite: 45 CFR § 164.520–528

11 HIPAA Complete Guide

Security Rule — overview

Effective 2005. Sets national standards for protecting electronic PHI (ePHI). Does not apply to paper or oral PHI (Privacy Rule covers those).

Three principles

Confidentiality

ePHI not available to unauthorized people or processes.

Integrity

ePHI not altered or destroyed without authorization.

Availability

ePHI accessible to authorized people when needed.

Three categories of safeguards

Administrative

People + policies — workforce training, access management, risk analysis, incident response, BAAs.

Physical

Buildings + devices — facility access, workstation policies, device disposal.

Technical

Software — access controls, audit logs, integrity controls, transmission security (encryption).

Cite: 45 CFR §§ 164.302–318. Implementation guide: NIST 800-66 Rev 2 (2024).

"Addressable" is not optional

Addressable means: implement it, OR document a reasonable equivalent. You cannot skip it. OCR specifically asks about addressable controls in audits — "we decided not to" is not a valid answer.

Cite: 45 CFR § 164.312

15 HIPAA Complete Guide

Required vs Addressable

Every Security Rule implementation spec is labeled Required or Addressable. Misunderstanding this is the most common HIPAA error.

required Required

Mandatory. Must implement exactly as specified. No alternatives accepted.

Example: Unique user identification, audit controls, BAAs.

addressable Addressable

Not optional. Must either:

  1. Implement the specification as stated, OR
  2. Implement a documented equivalent that achieves the same security objective, OR
  3. Document a reasoned explanation of why no implementation is needed (rare and risky)

Example: Encryption at rest, automatic logoff, integrity controls.

Best practice

Treat all addressable specs as required unless you have written, dated, signed justification for an alternative. Document the reasoning in your risk analysis. OCR sees "addressable" as a soft "do this anyway."

Cite: 45 CFR § 164.306(d)(3)

16 HIPAA Complete Guide

Breach Notification Rule

Effective 2009 (HITECH). Defines what counts as a breach and what to do when one occurs.

What counts as a breach?

Acquisition, access, use, or disclosure of PHI not permitted by the Privacy Rule that compromises security or privacy.

4-Factor Risk Assessment

  1. Nature and extent of PHI involved
  2. Who accessed or received the PHI
  3. Whether PHI was actually acquired or viewed
  4. Extent to which risk has been mitigated

Burden of proof is on you — default = breach.

Safe Harbor exception

If ePHI was encrypted per NIST standards and the encryption key was not compromised → not a reportable breach.

This is why encryption is the #1 control.

Notification requirements

ScopeAffected individualsHHSMedia
< 500 individualsWithin 60 daysAnnually (by Feb 28 next year)Not required
≥ 500 individualsWithin 60 daysWithin 60 daysProminent media outlets in the affected state/jurisdiction within 60 days

Cite: 45 CFR §§ 164.400–414

17 HIPAA Complete Guide

Business Associate Agreements (BAA)

BAA = the legal contract binding a Business Associate to HIPAA obligations. Without it, sharing PHI with a vendor = automatic violation.

A BAA must require the BA to

  • Use PHI only for the purpose stated
  • Implement appropriate safeguards
  • Report breaches to the Covered Entity
  • Pass HIPAA obligations to its own subcontractors via BAAs
  • Make PHI available to individuals exercising rights
  • Make books and records available to HHS for audit
  • Return or destroy PHI at contract termination
  • Use safeguards equivalent to Security Rule
  • Notify CE of unauthorized use/disclosure
  • Be directly liable to OCR (post-HITECH)

Vendor BAA reality (2026)

Will sign BAA

  • AWS, GCP, Azure
  • Stripe, Twilio, SendGrid
  • Auth0, Okta, Clerk
  • Salesforce Health Cloud

Enterprise tier only

  • Sentry, Datadog
  • Vercel, Netlify
  • Cloudflare
  • HubSpot, Intercom

Will NOT sign — replace

  • Google Analytics, Meta Pixel
  • Mailchimp, Postmark
  • Slack standard
  • Mixpanel, standard Heap
18 HIPAA Complete Guide

The Minimum Necessary Standard

Use, disclose, and request only the minimum amount of PHI needed to accomplish the intended purpose.

How it shows up in software

  • RBAC, not "all read": nurses don't see billing data; billing doesn't see clinical notes
  • API responses trim PHI: only return fields needed for the page/view
  • Logs scrub PHI: log user_id, not patient_name + diagnosis
  • Search excludes PHI columns unless authorized
  • Reports aggregate before exporting
  • Mobile push titles don't include diagnosis

Exceptions (minimum necessary does NOT apply)

  • Disclosures to providers for treatment
  • Disclosures to the individual themselves
  • Disclosures with patient authorization
  • Disclosures required by law
  • Disclosures to HHS for compliance review

Engineer rule of thumb

If you can't justify why this field/row/column needs to be visible to this user, it shouldn't be.

Cite: 45 CFR § 164.502(b), § 164.514(d)

19 HIPAA Complete Guide

De-identification methods

Two methods to convert PHI into non-PHI data. Once de-identified, HIPAA no longer applies.

Method 1: Safe Harbor

How: Remove all 18 identifiers (see slide 8).

Plus: Have no actual knowledge that remaining info could identify individual.

Pros: Mechanical, easy to verify, well-documented.

Cons: Can destroy analytic utility (dates aggregated to year, geography to state).

Most common method. Recommended for most use cases.

Method 2: Expert Determination

How: Qualified statistician applies generally accepted statistical principles to determine "very small" risk of re-identification.

Plus: Document methodology, analysis, conclusion.

Pros: Can retain analytic detail (dates, geography).

Cons: Requires expert. Expensive. Methodology must be defensible.

Use for research datasets where temporal/spatial detail matters.

Bonus: Limited Data Set (LDS)

Removes 16 of 18 identifiers but may retain dates and geographic info larger than street address. Used for research / public health / operations. Requires a Data Use Agreement (DUA), not full BAA.

Cite: 45 CFR § 164.514(b)–(e). HHS guidance: hhs.gov/hipaa/for-professionals/privacy/special-topics/de-identification

22 HIPAA Complete Guide

Common violations (OCR top findings)

Technical

  • Missing or weak encryption (at rest, in transit)
  • No MFA on remote access
  • Shared admin accounts
  • Unrestricted database access (no RBAC)
  • PHI in logs, URLs, push notifications
  • Third-party trackers on patient pages
  • Public cloud storage buckets
  • Unpatched systems
  • No audit logging or insufficient retention
  • Unencrypted backups

Administrative / Process

  • No risk analysis (or stale — top finding)
  • No designated Security/Privacy Officer
  • Workforce training missing or undocumented
  • Missing or outdated BAAs
  • No incident response plan
  • No documented sanction policy
  • Termination procedures not followed (ex-employees retain access)
  • No access reviews
  • Disposal policy missing or ignored
  • Mailing PHI to wrong patient (top type by count)

The single most common finding

OCR cites "failure to conduct an accurate and thorough risk analysis" in roughly 60% of all enforcement cases. Every other gap flows from not having a current one.

23 HIPAA Complete Guide

Implementation checklist (CTO view)

Foundation — the five things that block everything else

  1. Risk Analysis per NIST 800-30 — written, dated, reviewed annually
  2. BAAs inventoried + signed — every CE client, every subprocessor
  3. Encryption at rest + in transit — AES-256, TLS 1.2+, KMS keys
  4. Audit logs — every PHI read/write, immutable, 6-year retention
  5. Privacy + Security Officers designated in writing

Operational layer

  • MFA on all workforce + admin accounts
  • RBAC + minimum necessary access
  • Unique user IDs (no shared accounts)
  • Session timeout + auto-logoff
  • Workforce HIPAA training, annual, documented
  • Sanction policy + termination procedure
  • Incident response runbook + tabletop tested
  • DR / contingency plan + tested
  • Backups encrypted, offsite, restore-tested
  • De-identification for dev/staging data
  • Notice of Privacy Practices published
  • Patient access request workflow (30-day SLA)

Audit-readiness layer

  • Vendor BAA inventory + annual review
  • Annual security evaluation (technical + non-technical)
  • Penetration test annually
  • Patch management policy
  • Third-party SOC 2 or HITRUST audit (recommended)

Stacking rule

When multiple laws apply, follow the strictest one. HIPAA preempts state laws only when state law is less protective. State law can always be stricter.
